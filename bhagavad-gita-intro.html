Srimad Bhagavad Gita

This was a true story that happened long, long ago, during the end of Dwapara yuga, approximately 5000 years ago.

Dhrutharashtra was the brother of the late King Pandu, blind from birth. He had 100 children. Duryodhana was the eldest son of them all. Duryodhana was ruling the kingdom of Hasthinapura on behalf of his father. He was an arrogant and cunning person by nature.

King Pandu had five children only. Yudhishtira was the eldest of the Pandavas. He was also called Dharma Raja, by virtue of his nature. Bhimaseena, Arjuna, Nakula, and Sahadeva were Yudhishtira’s younger brothers. All these were well-disciplined and always lived truthfully in all respects. Draupadi was their wife. She was a pious lady and a sincere devotee of Lord Krishna.

Mischievous Duryodhana played a cunning game, which resulted in the Pandavas spending 12 years in the forest. They also spent one year in secrecy under the protection of King Virata. After finishing 13 years of exile, the Pandavas came out, and there were many negotiations for a peaceful settlement for their share in the kingdom.

The Kauravas did not agree at all with the Pandavas, and ultimately it resulted in a great war. That war became famous as the Maha Bharata War. It continued for 18 days. Eighteen akshauhinis of soldiers participated in that war. Out of them, 11 akshauhinis were with the Kauravas and only 7 akshauhinis were with the Pandavas. Lord Krishna also participated in that war as a charioteer for Arjuna, holding no weapons.

According to available sources, 21,870 chariots, 21,870 elephants, 65,610 horses, and 109,350 soldiers make one akshauhini. Then, a minimum of two people will be in each chariot—one to drive and the other to fight. Two warriors will be on each elephant, again one guiding the elephant and the other to fight. In each akshauhini there will be a minimum of 47,23,920 warriors, other than the animals. Alas!

You can imagine how many people participated in the whole of the Maha Bharata War. Unfortunately, by the end of the war no one was left alive among all the warriors, except the five Pandavas and just a handful of others here and there.

Bhishmacharya the Great was leading all the forces of the Kauravas. He was the grandfather of all the Pandavas and Kauravas. He was a bachelor and a very virtuous person. Dhrishtadyumna, the brother of Draupadi, was appointed chief commanding officer of the Pandavas’ army.

Before starting the war, Sage Vedavyasa the Great came to Dhrutharashtra to do some favour, if interested. Sage Vedavyasa asked him whether he wished to have sight to witness the war. Unfortunate Dhrutharashtra denied. But he requested the sage to grant the boon of witnessing all happenings of the war from his own place—called divya drishti—to his close companion Sanjaya. Sage Vedavyasa did so and left. Sanjaya was a charioteer for Dhrutharashtra and also his counsellor. He was a strong devotee of Lord Krishna, yet very honest in serving his master, the blind Dhrutharashtra.

The war started just before the first day of the month Margasira, according to the lunar calendar. While the war was about to begin, Arjuna was suddenly overwhelmed with sorrow. He refused to wage the war. As if to convince Arjuna and put him back on his duty—waging the war—Lord Krishna preached to him so many truths of life.

That sermon on the battlefield became famous as the Bhagavad Gita. This world-renowned scripture makes man dutiful, relieves all sorts of distress, and shows the right path when referred to with faith.

The Holy Bhagavad Gita, a celestial song with 700 slokas, was divided into 18 chapters. Each chapter is called an adhyaya. Each adhyaya is again named after some yoga. There are many meanings of the word yoga in Sanskrit. In the Bhagavad Gita, when naming the adhyayas, the word yoga is used to mean a “means”—a mode, method, or way. To be rid of misery in life, many yogas are recommended to the seeker. Our duty is to understand them and follow. We can overcome sorrows, lead a joyous life, and be successful in all our endeavours. The Holy Bhagavad Gita is thus relevant not only for now but forever.

Ten days passed. Dhrutharashtra was confident in his forces and did not ask for details of the war. But on the 11th day of the war, Dhrutharashtra received shocking news that their commander-in-chief, the great Bhishmacharya, had fallen on the battlefield. He was deeply moved, called Sanjaya to him, and began asking for details of the war.

Blessed with divya drishti, Sanjaya was able to perceive the whole state of affairs and began revealing each event to his master Dhrutharashtra. Though the Lord spoke the Bhagavad Gita on the very first day, no one knew of that preaching then—probably everyone was in a state of trance. It was brought to light only through Sanjaya on the 11th day, when King Dhrutharashtra asked him.

In view of that, the 11th day of the month Margasira is regarded as Gita Jayanti. Chanting the whole of the Bhagavad Gita is a blessing on this day. If that is not possible, reciting as many slokas as possible, or chanting at least a few hymns, also brings great good.

Thus began the Bhagavad Gita with a question from Dhrutharashtra.
